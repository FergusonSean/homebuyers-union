What a mortgage actually costs you

The sticker price on a house is a fiction. Here's what you're really paying.

10+ years
just to save the down payment

Median US home is around $420,000. A 20% down payment is $84,000. Saving 10% of an $80,000 income — $8,000 a year — means over a decade before you can even apply for a mortgage. And that's if nothing goes wrong.

$470,000
in interest on a $336,000 loan

A 30-year mortgage at 7% on a $336,000 loan costs $2,235 a month. Over 30 years that's $804,000 in payments on $336,000 borrowed. The bank takes $470,000 from you and gives you nothing in return.

$840,000+
total cost of a $420,000 home

Down payment plus interest: you'll pay twice the purchase price before you own anything free and clear. A $420,000 house costs you north of $840,000. Banks have structured the system this way on purpose.

There is another way. People have been doing it for centuries.

How it works

Six steps. Everyone ends up owning their home. The bank profits almost nothing.

  1. 1
    The group forms, everyone contributes. A cohort of members signs up and commits to a small monthly payment. This goes into a shared fund. Nobody has a house yet, so everyone's contribution is modest — roughly what you're already spending on utilities or a car payment.
  2. 2
    The fund hits the down payment target. Once the accumulated fund reaches the down payment threshold — typically 20% of the home price — the union buys the first house. The organization holds the title and takes on the mortgage. The first member moves in.
  3. 3
    The first member's contribution increases. They've moved in and no longer pay rent. Their monthly contribution goes up to reflect their new situation — still less than a solo mortgage payment. That higher contribution, combined with everyone else's, accelerates buying the next house.
  4. 4
    Each subsequent house is bought faster. Every time a member moves in, they start contributing more. The fund income grows. The interval between purchases shrinks with each cycle. The group builds momentum.
  5. 5
    All members are housed. Everyone is in a home. The union now holds the mortgages on all of them. Members continue contributing at the post-house rate — the money now goes entirely to paying off the mortgages ahead of schedule.
  6. 6
    The mortgages clear. The houses are gifted. The union dissolves. Once every mortgage is paid off, the union transfers full title to each member. The organization has served its purpose. It disbands.

Who this is for

This model is for renters. People who are doing everything right — working, saving, staying stable — and still watching homeownership recede into the distance because the down payment alone takes a decade to accumulate.

It's for people who understand that going it alone is the worst deal in the room. The housing market is designed to face you off against it by yourself — one income, one savings account, one offer, one set of terms. A union changes that. When you pool with others who are just as committed as you are, the math shifts in your favor for everyone.

This works because the people in it want it to work for each other, not just themselves. You get housed faster because others are contributing to the fund. They get housed faster because you are. That's not charity — that's a better structure.

This is also for people who want collective power over individual debt. The mortgage system is built to extract as much interest as possible from as many individuals as possible, each isolated, each negotiating alone. A union changes that dynamic. You are not one borrower — you are part of a group that the fund serves.

You don't need perfect credit. You don't need a large down payment saved. You need a steady income and the ability to commit to a monthly contribution for a long time. That's the contract.

If you've ever wondered whether there's a different way to do this — not an investment, not a gimmick, just people helping people buy homes — this is it.

What happens if something goes wrong?

The union is designed to be resilient. Three scenarios have clear, defined outcomes.

Member stops paying before move-in

The principal they contributed is refunded. The interest they would have earned — the time-value of their money held by the fund — is retained by the union as a penalty. This protects the group's timeline from being undermined by pre-move-in exits.

Member stops paying after move-in

The house is sold. Proceeds return to the fund. The union has a contractual obligation to maintain the mortgage, and a non-paying member in an organization-owned home cannot be allowed to drag the group down. This is handled directly and clearly.

Member chooses to move voluntarily

The departing member has two options. They can stop paying and allow the union to sell the house — proceeds go to the fund, clearing the mortgage and reducing the group's obligations. Or they can sell their membership spot on a secondary market, transferring their position in the queue and their contribution history to a new member. The second path lets them recover more of their investment and brings a committed replacement into the group.

What about home prices changing? This is a real risk that members take on together. If prices rise enough that a member can't find a home in the fund's target range, they have two options: drop out under the standard exit terms, or bring the issue to the group. The group can vote to raise the price target for remaining members — but that means higher contributions or a longer timeline, and everyone has to agree. In volatile markets, the better approach is to negotiate this upfront: the calculator's advanced options let you set a different home price for each member, so later positions already account for expected appreciation. Lock in those terms before anyone is housed.
